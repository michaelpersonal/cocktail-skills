# Core Tactics Modules

## Module: generate-mirrors

**Purpose**: Prepare mirror responses for likely statements from your counterpart.

**When to use**: Before a conversation where you need to extract more information without asking direct questions.

**Inputs needed**:
- What is the counterpart likely to say?
- What topics will come up?

**Method**:
1. List 5-7 things the counterpart is likely to say.
2. For each, identify the last 1-3 critical words.
3. Write the mirror as a question (repeat those words with rising intonation).
4. Note what information the mirror is designed to surface.

**How mirrors work**: Repeat the last 1-3 critical words (or a short phrase) with a curious, upward tone. It signals "tell me more" without a direct question. People instinctively elaborate when mirrored.

**Example output**:
```
They say: "We just can't move on pricing right now."
Mirror: "Can't move on pricing?"
Surfaces: Whether "right now" means ever, or whether there's a timeline.

They say: "The team has concerns about the timeline."
Mirror: "Concerns about the timeline?"
Surfaces: What specific concerns — capacity, quality, dependencies?

They say: "That's going to be a tough sell internally."
Mirror: "Tough sell internally?"
Surfaces: Who specifically needs convincing and what their objection is.

They say: "We've already committed to the board on this."
Mirror: "Committed to the board?"
Surfaces: How firm the commitment is and whether there's any flexibility.

They say: "I'd have to check with legal on that."
Mirror: "Check with legal?"
Surfaces: Whether this is a real constraint or a stall tactic.
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Mirroring every single statement (it becomes obvious and annoying)
- Mirroring with a flat or skeptical tone (should sound curious, not confrontational)
- Mirroring when you should be labeling (if emotion is present, label it instead)
- Using mirrors when the conversation is going well (don't slow down productive momentum)

## Module: generate-labels

**Purpose**: Prepare empathic labels for emotions and concerns you expect to encounter.

**When to use**: When emotions are likely to be present — frustration, anxiety, skepticism, defensiveness.

**Inputs needed**:
- What is the emotional landscape of this negotiation?
- What is the counterpart likely feeling?

**Method**:
1. List the emotions and concerns you expect.
2. For each, write a label using one of these stems:
   - "It seems like..."
   - "It sounds like..."
   - "It looks like..."
3. **Never say "I"** in a label. "I think you're frustrated" makes it about you. "It seems like there's some frustration here" makes it about them.
4. After delivering a label, **go silent**. Let them respond. The silence is where the real information comes.

**Example output**:
```
Expected emotion: Frustration about being asked to do more with less.
Label: "It sounds like you're being asked to absorb a lot without getting additional support."

Expected emotion: Skepticism about whether this is worth their time.
Label: "It seems like you've seen proposals like this before and they didn't pan out."

Expected emotion: Anxiety about setting a precedent.
Label: "It feels like there's a concern that saying yes here opens a door that's hard to close."

Expected emotion: Defensiveness about a decision they've already made.
Label: "It seems like a lot of thought already went into this approach."

Expected emotion: Pressure from above that they can't talk about openly.
Label: "It sounds like there are factors at play that make this more complicated than it might look."
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Labeling too often (one well-placed label is better than five rapid-fire ones)
- Labeling with the wrong emotion (if you guess wrong, they'll correct you — still useful, but don't guess wildly)
- Using labels to manipulate rather than to understand (people can tell)
- Following a label with an immediate ask (let the label breathe — silence after a label is where trust builds)

## Module: draft-calibrated-questions

**Purpose**: Prepare questions that guide the conversation without provoking defensiveness.

**When to use**: Any negotiation. Calibrated questions are the most versatile tool in this system.

**Inputs needed**:
- What problem are you trying to solve?
- What information do you need?
- What resistance do you expect?

**Method**:
1. Identify what you need from the conversation.
2. Convert each need into a "How" or "What" question.
3. **Never ask "Why"** — it sounds accusatory. "Why did you do that?" triggers defense. "What led to that decision?" opens a door.
4. **Never ask a question you know the answer to** — it feels like a trap.
5. Each question should make the other side work on your problem without feeling pressured.

**The core calibrated questions** (adapt for context):

| Purpose | Question |
|---------|----------|
| Surface constraints | "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with this?" |
| Force them to solve your problem | "How am I supposed to do that?" |
| Get implementation details | "How does this work on your end?" |
| Test commitment | "What happens if we can't get this done by then?" |
| Identify decision-makers | "Who else is involved in this decision?" |
| Slow down a rush | "What are we missing if we move this fast?" |
| Create options | "What would it take to make this work?" |
| Surface hidden objections | "What's holding you back?" |
| Reframe scope | "How does this fit into your bigger priorities right now?" |
| Build collaboration | "How can I help make this easier for you?" |

**Example output for a vendor negotiation**:
```
Need: Lower the price without damaging the relationship.
Bad question: "Can you do better on price?"
Calibrated question: "How can we make this work within our budget constraints?"

Need: Understand why they won't budge.
Bad question: "Why won't you move on this?"
Calibrated question: "What's driving the pricing on your side?"

Need: Get them to offer concessions.
Bad question: "What will you throw in?"
Calibrated question: "What flexibility do you have on the overall package?"

Need: Buy time to think.
Bad question: (awkward silence or premature agreement)
Calibrated question: "How should I be thinking about this?"
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Asking calibrated questions in rapid succession (it feels like an interrogation)
- Using "How am I supposed to do that?" aggressively (should sound genuinely curious, almost helpless)
- Asking calibrated questions when a direct question would be more respectful (don't be indirect about simple things)

## Module: run-accusation-audit

**Purpose**: Preempt every negative assumption, objection, and fear the counterpart might have about you or your ask.

**When to use**: Before any high-stakes ask, difficult proposal, or conversation where you expect resistance. Especially useful when asking for something big, delivering bad news, or entering a conversation where trust is fragile.

**Inputs needed**:
- What are you asking for?
- What might the counterpart think is wrong with your ask?
- What negative assumptions might they have about you or your motives?

**Method**:
1. Put yourself in their shoes. List every negative thought they might have:
   - About your ask ("This is too expensive / too risky / too late")
   - About you ("They're being greedy / political / unreasonable")
   - About the situation ("This is going to create more work / set a bad precedent")
2. Turn each into a statement that names it explicitly:
   - "You're probably thinking..."
   - "This might seem like..."
   - "You may feel that..."
3. Deliver these **before** you make your ask. This is counterintuitive — you're saying the bad stuff before they can. But it works because:
   - It shows self-awareness
   - It takes the power out of unspoken objections
   - It makes you seem reasonable, not defensive
4. After the audit, pause. Then make your ask.

**Example output**:
```
Situation: Asking for a budget increase mid-quarter.

Accusation audit:
"I know this isn't ideal timing, and you're probably thinking, 'Why is this coming up now when we already locked the budget?' You might feel like I should have planned better, or that approving this sets a precedent that makes every other team think they can ask for more mid-cycle. It might even seem like I'm not respecting the process. I want you to know I've thought about all of that, and I still think this is worth a conversation."

Then: make the ask.
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Listing too many accusations (3-5 is ideal; more than that sounds neurotic)
- Being sarcastic or dismissive ("I know you'll probably overreact" — no)
- Not actually believing what you're saying (if you can't say it sincerely, don't say it)
- Using the audit as a shield to avoid genuine accountability
