# Closing & Follow-Through Modules

## Module: detect-false-yes

**Purpose**: Determine whether you have a real commitment or a polite brush-off.

**When to use**: After any agreement, especially when the "yes" came too easily, too quickly, or without engagement.

**Inputs needed**:
- What was agreed?
- How did the conversation feel?
- Did they engage with the details or just agree in general?

**Method** (The Rule of Three):
1. **First confirmation**: The original agreement. ("Yes, we'll do it.")
2. **Second confirmation via summary**: Summarize the agreement and ask them to confirm: "So just to make sure I have this right — we're agreeing to [specific terms]. Does that capture it?"
3. **Third confirmation via implementation**: Ask about execution: "Great — what does implementation look like on your side?" or "Who do we need to loop in to make this happen?"

**Red flags for false yes**:
- They agree but won't discuss specifics
- They say "sure" or "that works" without engaging
- They agree to everything with no pushback (real agreements involve friction)
- They redirect to "let's discuss later" when you ask about next steps
- Body language or tone is disengaged (in live conversations)
- In email/Slack: response is short and noncommittal after a complex proposal

**When you detect a false yes**:
- Don't accuse: "It sounds like there might be something making this hard to commit to."
- Calibrated question: "What's the biggest obstacle to this actually happening?"
- No-oriented question: "Is this something that's just not going to work?"

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Calling someone a liar (they may genuinely intend to follow through and can't)
- Accepting the first "yes" and moving on without testing it
- Being so paranoid about false yes that you interrogate genuine agreement

## Module: identify-black-swans

**Purpose**: Uncover the hidden information that could change the entire negotiation.

**When to use**: In every negotiation, but especially when something doesn't add up — when their behavior doesn't match their stated position, or when you can't figure out why they're resisting.

**Inputs needed**:
- What is the negotiation about?
- What behavior or positions don't make sense?
- What questions have you been unable to answer?

**Method**:
1. **List what you don't know.** Not what you want — what you're *missing*:
   - What don't I understand about their position?
   - Why are they behaving this way?
   - What would explain this if I knew it?
2. **Look for three types of black swans**:
   - **Their constraints**: budget freezes, reorgs, legal issues, board pressure
   - **Their alternatives**: competing offer, backup plan, no real need to deal with you
   - **Their worldview**: they may define "fair" differently, or value things you haven't considered
3. **Surface black swans through**:
   - Face time (black swans rarely emerge over email)
   - Unguarded moments (before/after formal meetings, casual settings)
   - Calibrated questions: "What's the biggest thing I'm not seeing here?"
   - Mirroring unexpected statements
   - Asking their team members, not just the lead negotiator
4. **When you find a black swan**, recalibrate your entire approach.

**Example output**:
```
Situation: Vendor won't budge on price despite competitive alternatives.

What doesn't make sense: They know our budget range. They know we have alternatives. Why won't they move?

Black swan hypotheses:
1. They have a minimum margin requirement set by their board
2. They're already discounting from their standard rate and can't go lower
3. They have a pipeline deal that's more profitable, so they don't need our business urgently
4. There's a contract term they'd move on that's worth more to us than the price delta

Probing questions:
- "What would it take for this to work at our budget level, even if the package looks different?"
- "Help me understand the constraints on your side — what drives the pricing floor?"
- "Is there something about the deal structure that would make the numbers work better for both of us?"
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Assuming you know everything already
- Treating the counterpart's stated position as the whole truth
- Only looking for black swans that help you — also look for ones that change your approach entirely

## Module: create-closing-plan

**Purpose**: Design the end of a negotiation so the agreement sticks.

**When to use**: When you're approaching agreement and need to lock it down with real commitment.

**Inputs needed**:
- What has been agreed so far?
- What details still need to be confirmed?
- Who needs to approve?

**Method**:
1. **Summarize the agreement using their words.** People commit to their own language, not yours.
2. **Get the Rule of Three confirmation** (see detect-false-yes).
3. **Nail implementation details**:
   - Who does what?
   - By when?
   - What happens if something changes?
   - Who do we escalate to?
4. **Get it in writing within 24 hours.** Send a follow-up email or doc that captures the agreement. Frame it as "capturing what we discussed" rather than "here's the contract."
5. **Build in a check-in.** "Let's check in on [date] to make sure this is tracking."

**Example output**:
```
Closing plan for scope negotiation with product team:

Summary (in their language): "So we're aligned that Q3 will focus on the core payment flow, and the reporting features move to Q4. The team stays at current size, and we'll revisit headcount if Q3 delivery is on track."

Implementation details:
- Product updates roadmap doc by Friday
- I update sprint planning by next Monday
- We share updated timeline with stakeholders at next week's sync
- Check-in: Mid-quarter review on August 15

Follow-up email: Send within 24 hours, cc relevant stakeholders.
Escalation path: If scope creeps back, raise at weekly 1:1 with VP Product.
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Assuming a verbal agreement will hold without written follow-up
- Making the follow-up email sound like a legal document (keep it conversational)
- Not building in a check-in (agreements decay without maintenance)
