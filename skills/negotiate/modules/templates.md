# Negotiation Templates & Workplace Examples

## Template 1: Vendor Over Budget

**Situation**: Vendor proposal comes in 40% over your budget.

**Bad response**:
"This is way over budget. Can you do $X instead?"
*Why it fails: You anchor them to defense, lose information about what's flexible, signal desperation.*

**Better response**:
"Thanks for putting this together — I can see there's a lot of thought in the proposal. [Label:] It sounds like you've built in a comprehensive scope. [Calibrated question:] Help me understand what's driving the pricing — what are the biggest cost components? [Pause.] On our side, we're working within a budget that was set before we saw the full scope. [Calibrated question:] What would it take to make this work within a tighter budget, even if that means adjusting the package?"

*Why it works: You learn what's flexible. You let them solve the problem. You don't anchor to a number prematurely.*

## Template 2: Peer Asking for Too Much

**Situation**: A peer team wants your team to take on work without offering anything in return.

**Bad response**:
"We're already overloaded. You need to handle this yourself."
*Why it fails: Creates adversarial dynamic, doesn't address their need, makes you look uncooperative.*

**Better response**:
"[Accusation audit:] I know it probably feels like we should be able to absorb this — your team is under pressure and this touches our systems. [Label:] It sounds like this has become urgent on your side. [Calibrated question:] Help me understand the timeline you're working with and what's driving it. [Calibrated question:] How would you suggest we handle this given that we're already committed to [X, Y, Z] this quarter? [Redirect:] What if we mapped out the dependencies together and found a path that doesn't require pulling my team off their current commitments?"

*Why it works: You show empathy for their pressure, surface the real urgency, make them solve the resourcing problem with you, and redirect toward a joint solution.*

## Template 3: Leader Pushing an Unrealistic Timeline

**Situation**: Your VP says "I need this by end of month" and it's a six-week project.

**Bad response**:
"That's not possible. It's going to take six weeks."
*Why it fails: Flat refusal. No options. May sound like you're not committed.*

**Better response**:
"[Label:] It sounds like there's real urgency behind this — something's driving the timeline. [Calibrated question:] Can you help me understand what's making end-of-month critical? [Pause — listen for the real driver.] I want to make sure I'm solving the right problem. [If the driver is an external deadline:] What if we scoped a version that hits the critical parts by end of month, and completed the rest in the following two weeks? [Calibrated question:] What does 'done enough' look like for the audience or stakeholder who needs it?"

*Why it works: You find out WHY the deadline matters. Maybe they need a demo, not a finished product. The real deadline might be different from the stated one.*

## Template 4: Compensation Negotiation

**Situation**: You're discussing salary for a new role or a raise.

**Bad response**:
"I was hoping for something in the $180K range."
*Why it fails: Anchoring without rationale, no empathy for their constraints, easy to dismiss.*

**Better response**:
"[Accusation audit:] I'm sure you're thinking about budget constraints and internal equity, and I don't want to be tone-deaf about that. [Label:] It sounds like there are real constraints on your side. [Calibrated question:] Can you walk me through how comp decisions work here — what factors go into the range for this role? [After they share:] Based on what I've seen in the market for this role at this level — and the specific experience I'd bring [cite 1-2 specifics] — I was hoping we could talk about something in the range of $178,500. [Calibrated question:] How does that fit with what you're able to do?"

*Why it works: You acknowledged their constraints, learned their framework, anchored with a precise number backed by rationale, and gave them room to respond without feeling cornered.*

## Template 5: Scope Increase Without Budget

**Situation**: Partner team or client wants more work without more money.

**Bad response**:
"If you want more scope, we need more budget."
*Why it fails: It's a demand, not a conversation. Invites a power contest.*

**Better response**:
"[Label:] It sounds like the needs have evolved since we scoped the original agreement. [Calibrated question:] Help me understand what's changed and what's driving the new requirements. [Pause.] I want to make sure we're solving the right problem. [Calibrated question:] If we were to take this on, how would you suggest we handle the resourcing on our end? [No-oriented:] Would it be unreasonable to revisit the budget if the scope has genuinely changed?"

*Why it works: You treat the scope change as legitimate, make them confront the resourcing problem, and use a no-oriented question that makes "no, that's perfectly reasonable" the natural response.*

---

## Template 6: Conflict Over Priority or Ownership

**Situation**: Two teams claim ownership of a project or disagree on priority.

**Bad response**:
"This is clearly in our domain. Here's why."
*Why it fails: Triggers territorial defense. Both sides dig in.*

**Better response**:
"[Accusation audit:] I know this might seem like a turf battle — and I don't want it to be. [Label:] It sounds like we both care about getting this right, and we have different ideas about how. [Calibrated question:] What's the most important outcome here for your team? [Listen, mirror key phrases.] For us, the critical thing is [state your need clearly]. [Calibrated question:] What would a structure look like where both of those are protected? [Redirect:] How about we map out who does what based on where the expertise actually sits, rather than org chart lines?"

*Why it works: You name the dynamic, surface their real interest, share yours, and propose a principle (expertise over org chart) that depoliticizes the decision.*

# Realistic Workplace Examples (Before/After)

## Example 1: The Under-Leveled Job Offer

**Situation**: You received a job offer at Senior level, but your experience matches Staff level.

**Before**:
"I was expecting a Staff-level offer. My experience at [previous company] and my track record with [specific projects] put me well within that range. Can you reconsider the level?"

**After**:
"I'm genuinely excited about this team and the work — and I want to make sure we start this right. [Accusation audit:] You're probably thinking that leveling is tightly calibrated and I'm trying to negotiate my way up, and that's fair. [Label:] It sounds like there's a framework you use for leveling decisions. [Calibrated question:] Can you help me understand what the delta is between Senior and Staff in your rubric — what would I need to demonstrate? [Pause.] Because when I look at the scope of what you've described for this role — [specific project], [team size], [strategic impact] — and compare it with the Staff-level work I've been doing at [current company], it feels like there's a match. [No-oriented:] Would it be unreasonable to have the leveling committee take another look, given the specifics?"

**Why it's better**: You didn't argue. You asked them to show you their framework, which either reveals flexibility or gives you information for appeal.

## Example 2: Pushing Back on a Colleague's Bad Idea in a Meeting

**Situation**: A colleague proposes a technical approach you know will fail, and leadership seems interested.

**Before**:
"I don't think that will work because [lists technical reasons]."

**After**:
"[Label:] It sounds like you're trying to solve the latency issue without a major rearchitecture — I can see why that's appealing. [Calibrated question:] What happens to the system under peak load with that approach? [Mirror their answer.] [Then:] We ran into something similar last quarter with [specific reference]. [Calibrated question:] What if we mapped out the failure scenarios before committing? I want to make sure we're not surprised in production."

**Why it's better**: You didn't say "you're wrong." You showed respect for their intent, asked questions that guide them to discover the problem themselves.

---

## Example 3: Annual Review — Asking for a Raise

**Situation**: You believe you deserve a raise but your manager hasn't brought it up.

**Before**:
"I'd like to talk about comp. I've taken on more responsibility this year and I think my salary should reflect that."

**After**:
"I've been thinking about what the last year has looked like and I wanted to get your perspective. [Calibrated question:] How do you see my contributions relative to where I was a year ago? [Listen. Mirror. Label.] [Then:] I've been tracking some of the specific impact — [1-2 concrete examples with numbers]. [Accusation audit:] I know comp conversations are constrained by budget cycles and equity frameworks, and I don't want to put you in an awkward position. [Calibrated question:] What would it take to move the conversation forward on comp adjustment? [No-oriented:] Is there any reason an adjustment wouldn't make sense given [the impact data]?"

**Why it's better**: You let them assess you first, brought data without being adversarial, acknowledged constraints, and your closing question makes "no, there's no reason" the natural response.

## Example 4: A Client Changing Requirements Mid-Project

**Situation**: Client wants to add three features with no timeline or budget change.

**Before**:
"We can accommodate those changes, but we'll need to extend the timeline by three weeks and increase the budget by $40K."

**After**:
"[Label:] It sounds like some new priorities have emerged since we kicked off. [Calibrated question:] Can you walk me through what's driving the additions? [Listen — understand the real need.] I want to make sure we deliver what actually matters. [Calibrated question:] If we had to prioritize these against the original scope, what would you rank highest? [Calibrated question:] How would you like us to handle the impact on the timeline? [Summary:] So it sounds like [Feature A] is the must-have and [B and C] would be nice-to-have. What if we build A into the current phase, scope B and C for a fast follow, and keep the original timeline intact? [Implementation:] What does approval look like on your side for the follow-on phase?"

**Why it's better**: You found out what they actually needed, gave them ownership of the priority call, offered creative phasing instead of a flat demand, and moved to implementation.

## Example 5: A Skip-Level Meeting Where You Need Something

**Situation**: You have 30 minutes with your VP and need to make a case for investment in your team's tooling.

**Before**:
"I wanted to talk about our team's tooling needs. We've been operating with [tool] for two years and it's causing [problems]. I'd like to propose we invest in [solution]."

**After**:
"I know your time is tight, so I'll be direct. [Accusation audit:] You might be thinking this is another 'we need better tools' pitch — and I know you hear a lot of those. [Label:] It seems like there's real pressure right now to show output without adding cost. [One-liner case:] Our current tooling is adding roughly [X hours per sprint] in friction — I have the data if you want to see it. [Calibrated question:] If there were a way to recover that time without adding headcount, would that be worth a deeper conversation? [If yes:] Here's what I'd propose. [Keep it to 60 seconds, one clear ask, one clear tradeoff.] [Calibrated question:] What would you need to see to feel comfortable moving forward?"

**Why it's better**: You respected their time, preempted the "not another pitch" reaction, led with quantified impact, and closed with "what would you need" — which tells you exactly how to follow up.
