# Offer Handling Modules

## Module: respond-to-bad-offer

**Purpose**: Handle lowball offers, unreasonable demands, or positions clearly below your walkaway without burning the relationship.

**When to use**: When the counterpart opens with something unacceptable.

**Inputs needed**:
- What was the offer?
- Why is it unacceptable?
- What would be acceptable?
- Is walk-away a real option?

**Method**:
1. **Do not react emotionally.** Do not counter immediately. Do not say "that's insulting."
2. **Label the gap**: "It sounds like we're seeing this differently."
3. **Calibrated question**: "How did you arrive at that number?" or "What's driving that figure?"
4. **Mirror** the most revealing part of their justification.
5. **Let silence work.** After a calibrated question, wait. Do not fill the void.
6. If they hold firm:
   - Introduce your data calmly: "Based on [market data / comparable deals / our analysis], we were expecting something closer to [X]. Help me understand the gap."
   - Use the "how" pivot: "How would you suggest we bridge this gap?"
7. If they still hold:
   - Apply a no-oriented question: "Is it fair to say that there's no flexibility at all on this?" (Most people won't say "that's right, zero flexibility" — it sounds unreasonable even to them.)
8. If it's truly a dead end, walk away cleanly: "I appreciate you being straightforward. This doesn't work for us at this level. If anything changes, I'd love to revisit."

**Example output**:
```
Vendor quotes $200K for a $120K service.

Bad response: "That's way too high. We can't do more than $120K."
(Triggers anchor battle, puts you on defense.)

Better response:
"I appreciate you putting this together. [Pause.] It sounds like there's a lot of value in the package you've built. Help me understand how you're thinking about the pricing — what's driving the number?"

[Listen. Mirror key phrases. Label any concerns.]

"Based on what we've seen in comparable engagements, we were expecting something closer to $120-130K. How should we think about bridging that gap?"

[If they push back on flexibility]

"Would it be unreasonable to explore what the package looks like at a different price point?"
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Immediately countering with your number (you lose information advantage)
- Getting emotional or sarcastic
- Accepting a bad offer because the conversation is uncomfortable
- Threatening to walk away when you don't actually have an alternative

## Module: draft-counteroffer

**Purpose**: Construct a counteroffer that anchors effectively without damaging the relationship.

**When to use**: After you've gathered enough information from mirrors, labels, and questions to know what flexibility exists.

**Inputs needed**:
- What is their offer?
- What is your target?
- What is your walkaway?
- What non-monetary terms can you adjust?

**Method**:
1. **Use the Ackerman model** (adapted from Voss):
   - Set your target price/terms
   - Set your first offer at 65% of target (if you're buying) or 135% (if you're selling/asking)
   - Plan three incremental moves: to 85%, 95%, and 100% of target
   - Each concession gets smaller, signaling you're reaching your limit
   - On the final move, add a non-monetary concession to signal you're at the end
2. **Frame the counteroffer** using empathy, not demands:
   - Acknowledge their position
   - Present your counter with data or rationale
   - Ask a calibrated question to keep the conversation collaborative
3. **Never make a round number offer.** $127,500 signals precision and research. $130,000 signals you picked a number.

**Example output**:
```
Salary negotiation — they offer $145K, your target is $170K.

Move 1 (65% of the gap, aim for $161K):
"I really appreciate the offer, and I'm excited about the role. Based on my research into comparable roles at this level — [cite specific data] — I was hoping we could get closer to $161,500. What are your thoughts?"

Move 2 (if they counter at $150K, move to 85% — $165K):
"I hear you, and I understand there are constraints. Would $165,000 be something that works? I'm also flexible on [start date / review timing / sign-on structure]."

Move 3 (if they counter at $158K, move to 95% — $168K):
"I think we're really close. Could we land at $168,000? I'd also be glad to [commit to X / accept Y structure]."

Final (at target — $170K with non-monetary):
"$170,000 with a six-month review feels right. I can also be flexible on [PTO / start date]. I think that sets us both up well."
```

**Failure modes to avoid**:
- Going straight to your target number (you leave value on the table)
- Making equal-sized concessions (signals you have infinite room)
- Conceding without getting something in return
- Using the Ackerman model robotically without adjusting to conversational flow
